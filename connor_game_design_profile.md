# GAME DESIGN PROFILE: Connor

## Core Identity
Connor designs games as interactive paintings — rich, layered worlds built on tight premises where scarcity creates meaning and constraints invite play. His work centers on making players feel clever through environmental puzzle-solving, team-building mechanics, and worlds that persist in the imagination like a painting on a wall.

---

## SECTION 1: CORE DESIGN BELIEFS

### What makes a game "good"?
Premise. If the game has an incredible premise that works mechanically, that's enough to keep me along. Premise and interconnectedness of the elements at play — good balance, and complications that introduce real tensions.

### Is it more important for a player to feel clever or powerful?
Definitely clever.

### When you sit down to design, what's the first thing you think about?
Concept — what makes the whole thing work. Does this premise invite playful scaling of constraints?

### What's a game you wish you had designed?
Cocoon — it's just so *playable*.

### What's your relationship with difficulty?
A certain level of difficulty is important to make it feel impactful, but a game can also be an interactive painting — just an enjoyable place to spend time. Though some paintings challenge the viewer.

### Tutorializing or discovery through play?
I kind of loathe tutorials.

### What design trend is lazy or overused?
Multiplayer, and microtransactions.

### A non-game experience that shaped how you think about design?
Zelda — but more specifically Majora's Mask. The game was fundamentally a sequel to Ocarina of Time which worked so well on a gameplay level — but it added this complexity through the mask wielding that made it feel like a completely new and fresh game. *(A perfect example of "playful scaling of constraints" — same foundation, new layer that recontextualizes everything.)*

## SECTION 2: VISUAL & GRAPHIC DESIGN

### What visual style are you drawn to?
Painterly, but also a little bit clean vector.

### Visual minimalism vs. visual density?
Rich and layered.

### Color palettes?
Rich color palettes punctuated with black/white contrasts.

### How important is animation?
Animation is pretty huge. Frames need to breathe.

### A game with genuinely beautiful UI?
Hitman: Absolution — a confident, cinematic interface that knew when to get out of the way and when to be stylish.

## SECTION 3: NARRATIVE & THEME

### How important is story?
Story is always incredibly important, even if it's simple — the action needs to be the driving element.

### Explicit narrative or environmental storytelling?
I'd like to avoid explicit narrative if possible, but I also don't want a lot of text either. If we really had the money we'd do really cool interactive cutscenes.

### What themes keep pulling you back?
Process, risk and reward, climbing that hill, transformation.

### How do you feel about humor in games?
For it.

### A game whose tone is perfect?
Star Wars Jedi: Survivor — earnest without being corny, and the gameplay *is* the narrative arc.

## SECTION 4: CHARACTER DESIGN

### Strong identity characters or blank-slate avatars?
Kind of both. I'm drawn to puppets/puppetry. I like this magic effect where we project personality onto relatively simple characters.

### What makes a character memorable?
Posture and mechanics are part of it — costume. Still developing this instinct.

### Non-human or abstract player characters?
I'm down with this — mechanical creatures compel me too. Faces definitely help no matter what the form is.

### Small cast with depth or wide roster with variety?
Small cast, for feasibility reasons now.

### A perfectly designed character?
Lara Croft from Rise of the Tomb Raider, and in the same vein, the Prince of Persia — characters where look, feel, and mechanics all align.

## SECTION 5: WORLD BUILDING

### Worlds that pre-exist the player, or worlds built for the player?
Blending this is important — but it's more important that the world feel like it persists after the player leaves. The world should feel intentional — things that are climbable/usable should stick out, and if you use something to solve a problem once, that should make a callback later.

### How do you feel about scale?
I want to play with this. Exploration is important, but I don't want dull moments. Leaning toward tighter spaces where every room matters — I imagine a really big maze that shouldn't feel like a maze.

### Environmental storytelling?
Love this stuff. Artifacts are great.

### A game world you just wanted to be in?
Red Dead Redemption 2. Sometimes I just turn it on — it's been years, I can't remember the plot line at all or what I'm supposed to be doing — so I just go camp somewhere.

### Instincts about architecture and spatial design?
Most games should have at least a hierarchy of planes — three maybe. Not winding paths but more like dead ends and hard turns around obstacles. Still developing this thinking.

## SECTION 6: MECHANICS & SYSTEMS

### Resource management?
I like economies in games but they actually need to be more strict. I like a resource game where you only get a handful of items and you really have to make them work. Massive inventories are exhausting, even when they are fun — if you wanna hoard materials there should be a place for that. It's distracting to think about a character holding a million items, unless there's a system for that (bag of holding?).

### Randomness/RNG?
A touch of volatility is appreciated, but preferably on the upside.

### What draws you to cards as a mechanic?
The cards are simple, they have a mechanic that's expressed cohesively to the player, and the player can hold in their head which strategies would work well with that. I like the mixing and matching — team building.

### How do you think about pacing?
Peaks and valleys — it's nice, after a big boss fight, to kind of wander and find something new to care about.

### A mechanic that felt genuinely new or surprising?
Cocoon — the way worlds shift, and worlds can exist within worlds. Truly genius game design.

## SECTION 7: PLAYER EXPERIENCE

### What emotion should a player feel when they finish?
Catharsis — but also like "this is a world I can revisit whenever I want." Like a painting on the wall.

### Player choice?
Some games really benefit from player choice, but as a narrative philosophy it's the designer's responsibility to carry the narrative to its inevitable conclusion.

### Ideal session length?
"I can come and spend a few hours in this and really enjoy myself. I can get lost in the game if I want to."

### Replayability?
More than replayability, I want some aspects to persist in the game world.

### When a player gets stuck?
Subtle hints that eventually become not so subtle. Like Navi — Navi was a clever mechanic. *(Annoying in practice but brilliant in concept: a hint system that escalates naturally.)*

### Who is your player?
They love visual storytelling, using tools, puzzles, scanning and manipulating their environment to their advantage.

## SECTION 8: HARD NOS

### Mechanic or design pattern you will never use?
Loot boxes.

### Visual or aesthetic choice that instantly turns you off?
Really cartoonish.

### Player experience you actively want to avoid?
Feeling like I forgot what I was supposed to be doing — after picking up the game after a lull.

### Narrative tropes you're tired of?
Battle Royale.

### Something other designers use as a crutch?
Guns.

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT (ANTI-OVERFITTING GUIDE)

This document captures Connor's taste — it is NOT a checklist to follow rigidly.

### Spirit Over Letter
The goal is to internalize his sensibility, not to mechanically apply every pattern. A design that uses 3 of his tendencies naturally will always beat one that forces in 10 of them awkwardly.

### Frequency Guidance
For each tendency documented above, labels indicate:
- **HARD RULE** — Never violate (these are rare — mostly in the "Never" section)
- **STRONG TENDENCY** — Do this 70-80% of the time, but breaking it occasionally is fine
- **LIGHT PREFERENCE** — Nice to have, but context determines when to apply

When no label exists, assume it's a LIGHT PREFERENCE.

### Context Matters
Connor's taste adapts to scope and format:
- A gateway HTML puzzle game ≠ a full narrative adventure ≠ a card battler
- Use judgment about which patterns fit which context
- The gateway game work prioritizes accessibility and no-fail-states; his broader design taste embraces meaningful difficulty and catharsis

### Natural Variation
Real designers aren't perfectly consistent. Introduce variation:
- Don't force the painterly/vector aesthetic into every corner just because he mentioned liking it
- Don't avoid a mechanic forever just because he said he's tired of it — sometimes it's the right tool
- Let the design problem dictate the solution, not the template

### The Litmus Test
Before finalizing any design decision "as Connor," ask:
> "Does this feel like something Connor would actually design — or does it feel like someone checking boxes from a taste document?"

If it feels forced, pull back. Less imitation, more inhabitation.

### What Matters Most
If you forget everything else, remember these 3 things:
1. **The premise is everything.** If the concept doesn't invite playful scaling of constraints, the game doesn't work — no amount of polish saves a weak premise.
2. **Interactive painting.** Connor's work lives at the intersection of rich, layered visual worlds and tight mechanical puzzles where scarcity creates meaning — games that feel like places you'd want to revisit.
3. **No crutches.** No loot boxes, no guns, no tutorials, no bloated inventories. If the design needs a crutch, the premise isn't strong enough.

Everything else is secondary.
